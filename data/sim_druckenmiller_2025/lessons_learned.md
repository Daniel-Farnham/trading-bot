# Lessons Learned

## Lesson 2 [5]
When 3+ stocks in the same sector all show RSI 65+, MACD bullish, and OBV rising simultaneously, that is a sector rotation signal — institutions are moving money in systematically, not speculatively. Follow the institutional flow.

## Lesson 3 [5]
Known policy catalysts (inaugurations, elections, FOMC meetings) with clear directional outcomes are among the highest-conviction trade setups. When the mechanism is obvious (Trump lifts LNG ban → Cheniere benefits), size up before the event, not after.

---

## Lesson 4 [5]
For speculative/momentum-driven assets (crypto, meme stocks, high-beta momentum names), 'sell the news' dynamics are asymmetrically amplified. When a catalyst is fully priced in, even a neutral outcome (Trump not mentioning crypto in inaugural speech) is treated as a miss because the market had priced in the maximum bullish scenario. Rule: before holding a speculative position through a known catalyst event, ask 'what is already priced in?' — if the answer is 'everything,' the risk/reward flips negative regardless of whether the underlying thesis is valid.

## Lesson 5 [5]
When a sentiment shock hits a theme (e.g., DeepSeek hitting AI infrastructure), run three tests before exiting: (1) Is OBV rising or falling? Rising OBV during a price selloff = institutional accumulation, not distribution — hold. (2) Does Jevons Paradox apply? If the shock makes the underlying technology cheaper/more accessible, total end-demand often increases rather than decreases. (3) Is real-world capex confirming or contradicting the thesis? If OBV is rising, Jevons applies, AND capex deals are still being signed, the price move is noise. If all three point negative, the thesis is broken.

## Lesson 6 [3]
Supply-side government policy can be paradoxically bearish for commodity producers. Trump's 'drill baby drill' EOs increase domestic oil supply, which pressures oil prices lower and compresses E&P producer margins — the opposite of the intended thesis. Demand-side policies (LNG export terminal approvals enabling new foreign revenue) are unambiguously bullish for producers. Supply-side policies (drill more to lower domestic prices) are bearish. Before sizing into commodity producers on a government policy catalyst, classify the mechanism: if the government is trying to LOWER the commodity price, do not be long the producers of that commodity. Apply this test: 'Is the government trying to make this commodity cheaper for consumers?' If yes, the producers lose.

## Lesson 7 [3]
The DOGE/government-efficiency thesis is structurally double-edged for contractors: the same policy mandate that drives demand for efficiency platforms (PLTR as the data layer to find cuts) simultaneously shrinks the total addressable government budget. Before sizing heavily into government-revenue-dependent names on a policy thesis, model both the tool-provider uplift AND the budget compression headwind. When the government is the customer AND the cost-cutter, the net effect on contractor revenue is ambiguous — not unambiguously bullish.

## Lesson 8 [5]
When all positions within a single theme show synchronized triple distribution (below SMA50 + MACD bearish + OBV falling) in the same week — even with positive fundamental news (VST earnings) — this is a SECTOR ROTATION signal, not company-specific noise. The market is telling you institutions are exiting the theme regardless of fundamentals. The correct response: do NOT add to beaten-down theme positions; reduce or hold; deploy fresh capital into themes that ARE showing OBV rising. Adding to a theme in synchronized distribution is averaging down at the sector level, which amplifies losses if the rotation continues.

## Lesson 9 [5]
In a tariff-driven risk-off regime, healthcare names with domestic demand (LLY, NVO, JNJ — all showing OBV rising this week) act as genuine counter-cyclical hedges while AI infrastructure, growth tech, and financial deregulation names sell off in lockstep regardless of their fundamental quality. When tariffs are the dominant macro risk — as opposed to rate hikes or earnings misses — flight-to-safety flows into healthcare (tariff-immune: FDA-approved drugs generate revenue regardless of trade policy), consumer staples, and domestically-regulated utilities. Actionable rule: when tariff escalation appears imminent, stress-test each position for tariff exposure; add or increase healthcare positions BEFORE the trade war becomes consensus. Do not wait for the selloff to rotate — the rotation is the signal that you are already late.

## Lesson 10 [5]
Lesson 10: When you pyramid into a position using OBV rising as the primary technical confirmation, treat OBV reversal (rising → falling) as a conditional stop on the PYRAMID ITSELF — not necessarily the original position. The correct action is to reduce back to pre-pyramid size when OBV reverses, rather than holding the full pyramided position through the reversal. The original thesis may still be intact (and a scout position is warranted), but the technical basis for the oversized allocation is gone. Trigger conditions: any core position where (1) a pyramid entry was made with OBV rising as primary justification, AND (2) OBV reverses to falling within 1-2 review periods of that pyramid entry.

## Lesson 11 [5]
Lesson 11: 'Healthcare as tariff-immune' is an oversimplification that can lead to dangerous overconfidence in sizing. Pharmaceutical manufacturers (JNJ, LLY, VRTX) have three distinct layers of tariff exposure: (1) API (active pharmaceutical ingredient) imports — the majority of APIs are manufactured in India and China; (2) finished drug imports for products manufactured abroad; (3) indirect pressure if tariffs raise input costs industry-wide and compress reimbursement dynamics. The truly tariff-immune healthcare plays are domestic-service businesses with no import exposure (certain hospital networks, domestically-administered insurance). Trigger conditions: before sizing into any pharmaceutical manufacturer on a 'tariff-immune' thesis, classify the revenue exposure explicitly: where are APIs sourced, where is manufacturing located, and what fraction of revenue comes from domestically-manufactured finished goods.

## Lesson 12 [4]
Lesson 12: In a historic macro shock (tariff declaration, financial crisis, pandemic), the immediate response must be a supply-chain geography audit of every position. Classify each holding: (1) US-only revenue AND US-only operations = fully safe; (2) US revenue but imported inputs = exposed to input cost inflation despite demand being intact; (3) Global revenue with global supply chains = double exposure. Trigger: any single macro event causing SPX drop >3% in one session. Apply this classification BEFORE the next trading session, not at the next weekly review. A sound thesis in a company with 80% Asia-sourced manufacturing is not 'tariff-immune' — it is a cost-inflation story for the duration of the tariff regime. This lesson supersedes any narrative-level thesis quality.

## Lesson 13 [4]
Lesson 13: Managed care insurers (UNH, CVS) are structurally superior to pharmaceutical manufacturers as 'tariff-immune' healthcare plays. Managed care has zero supply chain exposure: premiums are US contracts, claims are domestic healthcare services, no API imports, no foreign drug manufacturing at any layer of the business. Pharmaceutical manufacturers (JNJ, LLY, VRTX) carry at least three tariff risk layers: API sourcing from India/China, finished drug imports, and input cost inflation. When building a healthcare defensive position during tariff shock, prioritize managed care for the largest allocation and treat pharma manufacturers as secondary with explicit API exposure discounts. Trigger conditions: any new healthcare position in a tariff escalation environment — classify as managed care (fully immune) or pharmaceutical manufacturer (partially exposed) before sizing.

## Lesson 15 [5]
Lesson 16: In a short position, MACD turning bullish while OBV continues falling is a dead cat bounce pattern, not a thesis-breaking cover signal. The primary signal hierarchy for short positions is: (1) OBV direction — is institutional distribution continuing? (2) SMA50 position — are institutions broadly exiting? (3) ADX — is the downtrend established and strong? (4) MACD direction — last in priority, most susceptible to short-term noise. When OBV is falling, price is below SMA50, and ADX confirms a strong downtrend, a MACD recovery reflects short-term price stabilization (short sellers taking profits, technical buyers nibbling), not genuine institutional re-accumulation. Covering a short solely because MACD turned bullish — while OBV and SMA50 still confirm distribution — is exiting a thesis on noise. Trigger conditions: any short position showing MACD turning bullish — immediately check OBV direction and SMA50 position before considering a cover. Only cover if at least two of the three primary signals (OBV, SMA50, ADX) have reversed.

## Lesson 16 [5]
Lesson 18: When mainstream financial strategists issue bubble warnings on a theme while OBV and institutional accumulation signals remain positive across the same names, trust institutional flow over strategist commentary. Strategist bubble warnings are often 6-18 months early in momentum regimes — they identify the eventual top correctly but are premature on timing. The actual exit signal is OBV reversal and SMA50 breakdown, not a strategist quote in financial media. Rule: any 'bubble warning' from a major bank strategist while OBV is still rising across the named sector — hold or add, do not reduce on the narrative alone. Only begin reducing when OBV reverses to falling.

## Lesson 17 [2]
New Lesson: Managed care operators (CVS, UNH) face a distinct domestic healthcare legislative risk — Obamacare subsidy policy, Medicaid expansion/contraction, and Congressional reimbursement decisions — that is entirely separate from tariff-immune classification. A position can be tariff-immune AND simultaneously exposed to domestic policy risk that is equally material to earnings. Before sizing into any managed care operator, classify both tariff exposure AND domestic legislative exposure: (1) Is ACA subsidy policy under active Congressional debate? (2) Are Medicaid rates subject to upcoming budget negotiations? (3) Is there a pending CMS reimbursement rule change? If any of these are active risks, treat managed care as policy-exposed domestically even when tariff-immune internationally. Trigger conditions: any managed care position (UNH, CVS, CI, HUM) when Congress has an active healthcare subsidy or reimbursement vote pending.
